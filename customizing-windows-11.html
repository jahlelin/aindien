Customizing Windows 11

Jason Moore

This is a guide on customizing Windows 11.

Windows 11 provides users with an extensive and highly adaptable array of options for customizing their digital workspace, allowing for a tailored computing experience that can suit a wide variety of personal preferences and professional needs.

The vast majority of these customization settings are conveniently centralized within the Settings application, which serves as the primary control hub for modifying the look, feel, and behavior of the operating system. It is important to note that these personalized settings are synchronized on a per-user basis, meaning that each individual who logs into the computer with their own account will have their own unique configuration saved and applied across devices where they sign in.

However, certain settings that are designed to apply universally to all users of a single machine—such as certain administrative or hardware-related preferences—are deliberately excluded from the current theme and are not part of the synchronized settings package. Furthermore, if you are using a local user account (as opposed to a Microsoft account that is connected to the cloud), your customization settings will not be synchronized with other computers or users, remaining strictly local to that specific device.

Among the most effective and visually impactful ways to personalize your Windows 11 experience are through the customization of the desktop background, the lock screen picture, and the selection of accent colors that appear throughout the interface.

One particularly dynamic option available to users is the ability to set up a slideshow of images, which cycles through a collection of pictures at an interval of your choosing, rather than displaying a single static image. For instance, the author of this text personally employs a folder containing space-themed imagery—featuring nebulae, galaxies, and planets—to serve as the source for such a slideshow.

In practice, using distinct folders for different themes—such as one folder for nature scenes, another for abstract art, and a third for family photos—greatly enhances your ability to rapidly switch between completely different visual moods and setups. All of these background-related adjustments can be found by navigating to the following path within the Settings application: Settings > Personalization > Background.

For those who prefer a more minimalistic or distraction-free environment, Windows 11 also allows you to choose a single, solid color as your desktop background. Some users find this approach cleaner and less visually cluttered, and the option to apply a solid color is available in the same Background section of the Personalization menu.

To adjust the color scheme more broadly across the operating system—affecting elements such as taskbars, windows, and Start menu backgrounds—you should go to Settings > Personalization > Colors. Within this section, you have the ability to toggle between light mode, which offers a bright and airy interface, and dark mode, which reduces eye strain in low-light environments and can also conserve battery life on certain display types.

Additionally, you can select any accent color you like; this accent color will be applied to buttons, title bars, and other interactive elements, giving your system a cohesive and personalized chromatic identity.

The lock screen in Windows 11 serves a critical security function: it prevents unauthorized access to your account whenever you step away from your computer, ensuring that your files, applications, and personal information remain protected. To quickly lock your screen at any moment—for example, before leaving your desk—you can press the Win + L keyboard combination, which immediately engages the lock screen without closing any of your open programs.

Once the screen is locked, you have the freedom to customize its appearance and behavior. You can add a custom picture to serve as the lock screen background, or alternatively, you can set it to show a slideshow similar to the desktop background. Moreover, you can specify which types of notifications—such as calendar reminders, email alerts, or messaging app previews—appear on the lock screen, and which are hidden for privacy. To adjust all of these lock screen settings, navigate to Settings > Personalization > Lock Screen.

A theme in Windows 11 is defined as a named and saved collection of settings that includes not only background images and color configurations but also system sounds (such as notification chimes and startup music) and mouse pointer styles (including cursors, selection icons, and busy indicators).

Windows 10 (the predecessor to Windows 11, but relevant here for context) includes a small but useful collection of ready-made themes out of the box, providing users with several distinct starting points. Additionally, the operating system provides a direct link to a much larger collection of free themes available for download from the Microsoft Store, where users can find themes based on games, movies, landscapes, abstract art, and more.

The page located at Settings > Personalization > Themes displays thumbnail previews of all themes that are currently installed on your system, allowing you to click on any thumbnail to apply that theme instantly.

Windows 11 also includes a vast and carefully designed selection of fonts and font families, each crafted to improve text legibility across all languages supported by the operating system, from English and Spanish to Chinese, Arabic, and Hindi. To view a complete list of every font installed on your computer, go to Settings > Personalization > Fonts.

At the top of this page, you will find a dropdown menu that provides an alternative method for installing new fonts: you can simply drag font files directly from File Explorer into the designated area, and Windows will automatically install them. Under the "Available Fonts" heading, you will see a series of preview boxes; each box displays the number of font faces (such as regular, bold, italic, and bold italic) available within a particular font family.

By clicking on any of these boxes, you can open a detailed view that shows a full list of all font faces in that family, along with additional metadata, including the font version number and the name of the designer or foundry that created it.

For users who prefer to work at high screen resolutions—such as on 4K or ultra-wide monitors—Windows 11 provides scaling options that make text, icons, and interface elements easier to see and interact with.

You can enlarge the display of different applications, zoom in on specific parts of the screen, and adjust scaling settings to ensure that everything remains readable without forcing you to lean closer to the monitor. These scaling options can be found within the Display Settings section of the operating system.

It is worth noting, however, that there is no officially supported method to change the core system font in Windows 11 (the default typeface used for menus, dialog boxes, and system messages). Nevertheless, you can still increase or decrease the size of text everywhere across the system—in File Explorer, settings windows, dialog boxes, and more—without changing the font itself. To do so, go to Settings > Accessibility > Text Size, and then use the slider to adjust the text size to a level that is comfortable for your vision and working environment.
